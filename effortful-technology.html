Effortful Technology

November 2025

The mid-20th-century rise of television marked a profound shift in modern life, transforming leisure from active pursuits like repairing tools, composing letters, or mastering instruments to passive viewing. Before TV's dominance, free time demanded creativity and effort; afterward, it became a sedentary ritual of consumption, eroding the rhythm of personal involvement.

As Neil Postman argued in Amusing Ourselves to Death, television didn't merely alter content—it reshaped attention itself, prioritizing spectacle over substance and training audiences to crave constant stimulation rather than deep reflection. This cultural pivot set the stage for later technologies, where the internet and social media intensified the trend, delivering endless, frictionless distractions that fragment focus and discourage sustained thought.

Today, what once required deliberate effort arrives with effortless swipes and streams. Entertainment, formerly a rare event, now hums as a perpetual backdrop, fostering an obsession with comfort that thinkers like Paul Bloom warn against in The Sweet Spot: The Pleasures of Suffering and the Search for Meaning. Bloom and others contend that this pursuit of ease undermines core human qualities: discipline, curiosity, and resilience, as passive consumption supplants the small challenges that build character.

Consequently, the routine practice of effort—tackling daily puzzles, fixing minor issues, or engaging deeply—has faded from everyday routines. We labor intensely at work but surrender downtime to seamless streams of content, scrolling instead of creating, binge-watching rather than building, reacting rather than reasoning. Over years, this imbalance dulls not just our skills but our self-perception and social bonds.

These habits ripple into relationships, diminishing our capacity for genuine connection. As Sherry Turkle explores in Alone Together, mediated interactions via devices foster isolation, making us less adept at empathy and face-to-face engagement. A 2018 University of Pennsylvania study led by Melissa Hunt reinforced this, finding that capping social media at 30 minutes daily markedly reduced loneliness and depression, underscoring how digital overuse directly harms emotional health. Follow-up research echoes these benefits, showing that even brief reductions in screen time alleviate anxiety, stress, and boost well-being.

Humans excel at what they repeatedly practice, a principle rooted in neuroplasticity—the brain's ability to rewire through experience. Actively embracing effort strengthens neural pathways for focus and problem-solving, while habitual passivity fosters inertia and cognitive dullness. Studies confirm that active engagement, whether physical or mental, enhances brain function and overall well-being far more than passive activities like endless scrolling. Each moment of avoidance isn't idle; it's reinforcing a cycle of disengagement.

Reversing this isn't about ditching technology—it's too woven into work, communication, and cognition—but reclaiming intentional use. Prioritize production over consumption by incorporating daily challenges: solving puzzles, writing reflections, or pursuing hobbies that demand focus. Swap aimless browsing for tools that build skills, nurture social ties, and foster family bonds, turning screens into allies rather than escapes.

That's the vision behind The Almanac: a platform to revive effortful habits through healthier screen time that sharpens focus and encourages shared experiences. Designed for individuals and families alike, it transforms digital moments into opportunities for connection—parents and children learning together, screens sparking dialogue instead of division.

Ultimately, technology needn't be the enemy; it's a tool to wield mindfully. By curating better habits—limiting passive apps, embracing active ones—we nourish minds and relationships. Recovery begins modestly: five to twenty minutes daily on demanding tasks rebuilds the "effort muscle," proving that incremental acts accumulate into profound change.
