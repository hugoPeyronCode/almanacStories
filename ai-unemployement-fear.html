The Fear is Not New. The Speed Is.

December 2025

Has humanity feared technological unemployment before?

Yes, repeatedly.

1811–1816. English Luddites destroy textile looms. Parliament responds with the Frame Breaking Act (1812), making machine destruction punishable by death.

1831–1834. Canuts revolt in Lyon against Jacquard looms. 600 dead. Army intervention required.

1890s. Electricity eliminates entire professions. New York counted 1,000 lamplighters in 1880. Zero by 1920.

1930. Keynes predicts a 15-hour workweek by 2030 due to automation.

1961. TIME Magazine runs the cover story "Automation: Will It Kill Jobs?" Economist Wassily Leontief compares workers to horses made obsolete by cars. For context: the US horse population dropped from 26 million in 1915 to 3 million in 1960.

1995. Jeremy Rifkin publishes The End of Work, predicting mass technological unemployment.

Each time the pattern repeated: destruction, then creation, then adaptation. The US agricultural workforce went from 40% in 1900 to 2% in 2000. No permanent mass unemployment followed.

Is AI different this time?

Arguments for "yes." AI targets cognitive work, the very refuge displaced workers were told to flee toward. Goldman Sachs estimated in 2023 that 300 million jobs are exposed globally. The speed is unprecedented. GPT-3 to GPT-4 took 18 months. ChatGPT reached 100 million users in 2 months. TikTok needed 9 months for the same milestone. AI substitutes rather than augments. A mechanical loom still needed an operator. In 2024, Klarna replaced 700 customer service agents with AI. IBM paused hiring for 7,800 back-office roles.

Arguments for "no." New jobs always emerge unpredictably. "App developer" did not exist before 2008. Today, the US counts over 2 million of them. AI still hallucinates. GPT-4 shows a 15–20% error rate on factual tasks. Human oversight remains necessary. Every catastrophic prediction has been wrong. US unemployment in 2024 hovers around 4%, near historic lows, despite decades of automation.

Conclusion

The difference is not necessarily more job destruction. It is faster destruction, hitting people who believed they were safe.

The economic challenge is less about total volume than about the brutality of the transition and society's capacity to retrain at AI speed.
